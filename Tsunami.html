Tsunami

A. Definition of Tsunami

       A tsunami is a series of waves caused by a sudden movement of ocean water. Most tsunamis are caused by large earthquakes under the ocean floor. Landslides, volcanic eruptions, certain weather events, and meteorites can also cause tsunamis. These waves are driven by gravity and can travel very far. Tsunamis are very powerful and dangerous. The inner core’s heat helps drive movement in the liquid outer core. This movement creates energy that moves Earth’s tectonic plates. If the plates collide, earthquakes will happen on the ocean floor. As the ocean floor rises or drops, so does the water above it. If this movement is strong enough, a tsunami radiates in all directions.

B. Types of Tsunami

       Tsunamis can be classified into three types based on the distance from their source and the time they take to reach the coast. A local tsunami originates within about 100 km and can strike in less than an hour. If warnings are not issued immediately, many people may lose their lives. Regional tsunamis come from sources up to 1,000 km away and arrive within 1–3 hours. If residents evacuate quickly, the damage and casualties can be reduced. Tele-tsunamis, also called ocean-wide or distant tsunamis, are generated more than 1,000 km from the coast. These tsunamis can cross entire ocean basins and still cause destruction far from their origin. Even though they are rare, major earthquakes often trigger them, affecting multiple countries at once.

C. Stages of Tsunami

       Tsunamis have three main stages: wave creation, wave travel, and wave run-up on land. Earthquake tsunamis usually begin when the seafloor moves upward or downward. Landslides and volcanic eruptions can also create tsunamis. There are many factors that determine a tsunami's size. The amount of movement of the ocean floor, the size of the area over which it occurs and the depth of the water are all factors that determine a tsunami's size. If a tsunami reaches shallow coastal areas, its waves become higher and more destructive. Tsunamis can cause severe damage to coastal communities. Because tsunamis are powerful and unpredictable, early warning systems are very important. If it occurs near the shore, waves will arrive within minutes and before any warning. While barely noticeable in the open ocean, waves build in height as they approach the shore. In a few cases, more than one wave can be expected roughly every 5 to 60 minutes.
